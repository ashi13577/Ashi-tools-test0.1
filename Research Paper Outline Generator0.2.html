0. Research Fundamentals

What is Research?

Research is a systematic investigation to establish facts, solve problems, or create new knowledge. It involves collecting, analyzing, and interpreting information to answer questions or test hypotheses.

Types of Research

  • Basic (Pure) Research: Aims to expand knowledge, regardless of immediate application.
  • Applied Research: Aims to solve practical problems.
  • Quantitative Research: Focuses on numerical data, statistics, and objective measurement (e.g., surveys, experiments).
  • Qualitative Research: Focuses on understanding experiences, perspectives, and meanings (e.g., interviews, case studies, ethnography).
  • Mixed Methods Research: Combines quantitative and qualitative approaches.
  • Exploratory Research: Investigates a problem that is not clearly defined.
  • Descriptive Research: Describes the characteristics of a population or phenomenon.
  • Explanatory Research: Explains the relationship between variables.

The Research Process

  1. Identify a Problem: Define the research gap or question.
  2. Review Literature: Understand existing knowledge and theories.
  3. Formulate Objectives/Hypotheses: State what you aim to achieve or test.
  4. Design Methodology: Plan how to conduct the research.
  5. Collect Data: Gather information using chosen methods.
  6. Analyze Data: Process and interpret the collected data.
  7. Interpret & Report Findings: Draw conclusions and communicate results.

4. Methodology - Research Design Structure

Understanding Research Design

Research design is the framework of research methods and techniques chosen by a researcher. The design allows researchers to hone in on research methods that are suitable for the subject matter and set up their studies for success.

Key Components of Research Design:

  • Research Philosophy: Your beliefs about how data should be collected, analyzed, and used (e.g., Positivism, Interpretivism).
  • Approach to Theory Development: Deductive (testing theory) or Inductive (building theory).
  • Research Strategy: Overall plan (e.g., Experiment, Survey, Case Study, Ethnography, Action Research).
  • Choice of Methods: Mono-method, Multi-method, Mixed Methods.
  • Time Horizon: Cross-sectional (snapshot at one point) or Longitudinal (over a period).

7. References & Citing

Why Cite?

Citing sources properly is crucial in academic research to:

  • Give credit to original authors.
  • Avoid plagiarism.
  • Allow readers to locate the sources.
  • Demonstrate your knowledge of the field.
  • Strengthen your arguments with evidence.

Common Citation Styles:

  • APA (American Psychological Association): Widely used in social sciences, education, and psychology. Focuses on author-date system.
  • MLA (Modern Language Association): Popular in humanities (literature, languages). Focuses on author-page number.
  • Chicago (The Chicago Manual of Style): Flexible, often used in history and arts. Offers notes-bibliography and author-date systems.

Tips for Managing References:

  • Start early: Record full citation details as you conduct your literature review.
  • Use reference management software (e.g., Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote) for efficiency.
  • Be consistent: Stick to one citation style throughout your paper.
  • Double-check: Ensure all in-text citations have a corresponding entry in your reference list.
